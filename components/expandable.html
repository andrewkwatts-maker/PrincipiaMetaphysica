The Birth of Athena

3 Primary Sources

Athena sprang fully formed from Zeus's head after he swallowed her mother Metis.

Hesiod's Theogony (lines 886-900)

"Zeus himself gave birth from his own head to bright-eyed Tritogeneia, the awful, the strife-stirring, the host-leader, the unwearying, the queen, who delights in tumults and wars and battles."

Homer's Iliad (Book 5.875)

"She sprang from the head of aegis-bearing Zeus, full-armed in gleaming gold."

Pindar's Olympian Ode 7

"Athena leapt from the top of her father's head with a mighty shout."

FAQ Accordion

Greek mythology is the body of myths originally told by the ancient Greeks, concerning their gods, heroes, and the nature of the world.

The Olympian gods were the principal deities of the Greek pantheon, residing atop Mount Olympus. The twelve major Olympians were Zeus, Hera, Poseidon, Demeter, Athena, Apollo, Artemis, Ares, Aphrodite, Hephaestus, Hermes, and either Hestia or Dionysus.

The Titanomachy was a ten-year series of battles fought between the Titans, led by Cronus, and the Olympian gods, led by Zeus. The war ended with the victory of the Olympians and the imprisonment of most Titans in Tartarus.

Multi-Level Expansion

The Greek pantheon consists of multiple generations of deities.

The primordial deities emerged at the beginning of creation: Chaos, Gaia, Tartarus, Eros, Erebus, and Nyx.

The Titans were the second generation of divine beings: Cronus, Rhea, Oceanus, Tethys, Hyperion, Theia, and others.

The Olympian gods overthrew the Titans and became the principal deities of Greek religion.
