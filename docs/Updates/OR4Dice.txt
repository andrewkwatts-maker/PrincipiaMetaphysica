The GRW model (Ghirardi-Rimini-Weber) is one of the most influential dynamical collapse theories in quantum mechanics. Proposed in 1986 by GianCarlo Ghirardi, Alberto Rimini, and Tullio Weber, it aims to solve the measurement problem by modifying the Schrödinger equation to include spontaneous, random wavefunction collapses. Unlike standard quantum mechanics (where collapse is observer-induced or undefined), GRW makes collapse an objective, physical process — no need for observers or many-worlds branching.
It's "continuous spontaneous localization" (CSL) in its modern form, but GRW is the original discrete version. The model is testable, falsifiable, and remains active in quantum foundations research (2024–2026 updates focus on experimental bounds and adiabatic versions).
Core Idea
In standard QM, superpositions persist indefinitely until "measured." GRW adds a stochastic term: the wavefunction occasionally "hits" a random point in space, localizing particles with Gaussian probability. This suppresses macroscopic superpositions (e.g., Schrödinger's cat) while leaving microscopic ones intact.
