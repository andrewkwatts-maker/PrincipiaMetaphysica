The crux of the hierarchy problem lies in the radiative corrections to the Higgs mass.  In the Standard Model (SM), the Higgs mass squared, m_H^2, receives contributions from loop diagrams involving all particles that couple to the Higgs. Supersymmetry provides a mechanism to alleviate this problem. For every boson in the SM, SUSY postulates the existence of a fermionic superpartner with the same mass and couplings. If this fermion has a bosonic superpartner (a sfermion) with the *same* mass and couplings, the sfermion loop contributes a positive term, +λ_f^2 Λ^2.  The quadratic divergences cancel *exactly* due to the opposite statistics and identical couplings.

Mathematically, this can be expressed as follows. Let  δm_H^2 be the radiative correction to the Higgs mass squared.  In the absence of SUSY, we have:

δm_H^2  ∝ Λ^2

With SUSY, the contributions from particles and their superpartners cancel:

where m_f and m_s are the masses of the fermion and sfermion, respectively, and m is a typical mass scale in the theory.  Crucially, the quadratic divergence is absent, replaced by a logarithmic divergence. **1. Mathematical Mechanism of KKLT**

The KKLT mechanism, primarily developed in the context of type IIB string theory compactifications on Calabi-Yau manifolds, aims to stabilize moduli fields and generate a small cosmological constant, thereby addressing the hierarchy problem indirectly. *   **Superpotential:** The superpotential is the heart of the mechanism, given by:

    *W* = *W*<sub>0</sub> + *A* exp(-*aT*)

    Here:

    *   *W*<sub>0</sub> is a constant term arising from flux quantization in the compactified space. This is crucial.
    *   *A* is a constant prefactor, often related to the number of D-branes involved in the non-perturbative effects.

    *   exp(-*aT*) represents a non-perturbative correction to the superpotential, arising from gaugino condensation in a hidden sector gauge theory, or, more generally, from Euclidean D-brane instantons wrapping cycles in the compactification manifold.

*   **Scalar Potential:** The scalar potential *V* is derived from the superpotential and Kähler potential using the standard supergravity formula:

    *V* = e<sup>*K*</sup> ( *K*<sup>T T̄</sup> *D*<sub>T</sub>*W* *D*<sub>T̄</sub>*W̄*  - 3 |*W*|<sup>2</sup> )

    where *D*<sub>T</sub>*W* = ∂*W*/∂*T* + (*∂K*/∂*T*)*W* is the Kähler covariant derivative, and *K*<sup>T T̄</sup> is the inverse of the Kähler metric.

*   **AdS Minimum:** Substituting the KKLT superpotential and Kähler potential into the expression for *V*, one finds that a minimum of the potential exists at a value *T*<sub>0</sub>, where the derivative of the potential with respect to *T* is zero. ## 8. Relaxion Mechanism

## Relaxion Mechanism in the Principia Metaphysica Context: A Critical Analysis

The hierarchy problem, the vast disparity between the electroweak scale (represented by the Higgs VEV, *v*) and the Planck scale (M_Pl), remains a central challenge in particle physics. The Principia Metaphysica (PM) framework, with its unique dimensional chain (26D -> 13D -> 7D -> 4D), G2 holonomy compactification, and specific constants, offers a novel context to explore potential solutions. This analysis focuses on the relaxion mechanism as a possible solution, examining its applicability and viability within the PM framework.

**1. Mathematical Mechanism of the Relaxion:**

The relaxion mechanism, introduced by Graham, Kaplan, and Rajendran (GKR, Phys. Rev. Lett. 115, 221801 (2015)), postulates a pseudo-Nambu-Goldstone boson (pNGB) field, *phi*, that dynamically scans the Higgs mass squared, *m_H^2*, during inflation. The Lagrangian typically includes:

*   **Higgs Potential:**  V(H, phi) = (-M^2 + g phi) |H|^2 + lambda |H|^4 + V(phi)
    *   *M* is a cutoff scale, potentially related to the PM's inherent scales.
    *   *g* is a coupling constant controlling the *phi*-dependent shift in *m_H^2*.
    *   *lambda* is the Higgs quartic coupling.
    *   V(phi) is a potential for the relaxion, often including a small explicit breaking term.

*   **Relaxion Potential:** V(phi) = Lambda^4 cos(phi/f)
    *   *Lambda* is a dynamically generated scale.
    *   *f* is the relaxion decay constant.

The core idea is that during inflation, the Hubble friction slows down the evolution of *phi*. As *phi* rolls down its potential, it scans the value of *m_H^2*. Initially, *m_H^2* is positive (fine-tuned to be close to the cutoff). As *phi* increases, *m_H^2* decreases, eventually becoming negative.

The scanning process is halted when *m_H^2* crosses zero. This triggers electroweak symmetry breaking (EWSB), leading to a non-zero Higgs VEV. EWSB generates a barrier in the *phi* potential, typically through non-perturbative effects related to QCD. This barrier traps the relaxion at a value of *phi* that yields a small, technically natural, value for *m_H^2*.

The barrier is generated by terms like:

*   V_barrier(phi) ~ Lambda_QCD^4 cos(phi/f - theta)
    *   Lambda_QCD is the QCD scale.
    *   *theta* is a CP-violating phase.

The relaxion stops rolling when the slope of the potential, *g v^2*, balances the barrier height, Lambda_QCD^4. This leads to the approximate condition: *g v^2 ~ Lambda_QCD^4*. This is the key to solving the hierarchy problem. The smallness of *g* allows a naturally small Higgs mass.

**2. Application to the PM Framework:**

Within the PM framework, the relaxion mechanism faces both opportunities and challenges.

*   **Axionic Moduli as Relaxions:** The G2 holonomy compactification on a 7-manifold in PM naturally leads to axionic moduli. Their potential is protected by shift symmetries, making them naturally light.  The decay constant *f* could be related to the compactification scale or string scale, potentially alleviating the need for super-Planckian field excursions which often plague the original relaxion models.

*   **Cutoff Scale M:** The cutoff scale *M* in the Higgs potential is a critical parameter. In PM, a plausible candidate for *M* is the Kaluza-Klein scale associated with the 7-manifold compactification, or even the 13D Planck scale.  This scale is likely to be significantly lower than the 26D Planck scale, potentially easing constraints on the relaxion parameters. The key constant *b3* = 24, reflecting the topology of the compactified space, could influence the effective cutoff scale.

*   **QCD Barrier Generation:** The generation of the QCD barrier presents a challenge. The standard relaxion model relies on the *theta* angle in QCD. However, the strong CP problem demands that *theta* be extremely small. PM's shadow sector, with its 163 degrees of freedom, could play a crucial role.  Perhaps the shadow sector has its own non-Abelian gauge group which becomes strong at a scale Lambda_Shadow, and contributes to the barrier.  This could alleviate the reliance on the QCD *theta* angle.

*   **Inflationary Embedding:** PM's cosmological framework needs to accommodate the long period of inflation required by the relaxion mechanism. The dimensional chain 26D -> 13D -> 7D -> 4D could have implications for the inflationary era. The early stages of dimensional reduction might be linked to inflation itself. The G2 holonomy compactification might also influence the inflaton potential.

**3. Specific Calculations & Constraints:**

Let's consider a simplified calculation assuming the relaxion is an axionic modulus originating from the G2 compactification.  We assume that the cutoff scale *M* is related to the Kaluza-Klein scale, M ~ M_KK.  We further assume the shadow sector contributes to the barrier, with Lambda_Shadow ~ 100 GeV.

1.  **Hierarchy Condition:**  g v^2 ~ Lambda_Shadow^4, leading to  g ~ Lambda_Shadow^4 / v^2 ~ (100 GeV)^4 / (246 GeV)^2 ~ 1.66 x 10^-4 GeV^2.

H_I < Lambda_Shadow^2 / M_Pl ~ (100 GeV)^2 / (2.435 x 10^18 GeV) ~ 4.1 x 10^-15 GeV.  This is a very low Hubble scale, requiring a long period of inflation. The relaxion mechanism offers a compelling approach to the hierarchy problem within the PM framework. The existence of axionic moduli from G2 compactification provides a natural candidate for the relaxion field. However, significant challenges remain:

The relaxion mechanism, particularly utilizing axionic moduli within the G2 compactification, offers a promising avenue for addressing the hierarchy problem in the PM framework. **1. The Mathematical Mechanism of Clockwork**

The Clockwork mechanism, initially proposed in [1,2], provides an elegant way to generate exponentially small couplings from a series of hierarchical interactions. The core idea involves introducing a chain of N scalar fields, ϕ_i (i = 0, 1, ..., N), with nearest-neighbor interactions.  The Lagrangian typically takes the form:

```
L = 1/2 Σᵢ (∂µϕᵢ)² - 1/2 m² Σᵢ ϕᵢ² - 1/2 (M² Σᵢ (ϕᵢ - q ϕᵢ₊₁)² )
```

Here:

*   `m` is a mass scale, often taken to be of order the electroweak scale.
*   `M` is a heavier mass scale.
*   `q` is a dimensionless parameter (often assumed to be q > 1) that governs the strength of the mixing between adjacent fields.

The key is the `M² (ϕᵢ - q ϕᵢ₊₁)²` term.  After diagonalization, the mass matrix yields a light mode, π, which is a linear combination of the original fields:

```
π = Σᵢ fᵢ ϕᵢ
```

Crucially, the coupling of this light mode to the Standard Model (SM) sector (e.g., the Higgs) is exponentially suppressed.  Specifically, the coefficient associated with the zeroth field, f₀, which might be the field directly coupled to the Higgs, is suppressed by a factor of approximately `q⁻ᴺ`.

**Mathematical Details:**

The mass matrix arising from the Lagrangian is a tridiagonal matrix.  Diagonalizing this matrix leads to a set of N+1 mass eigenstates, one of which is much lighter than the others when `q` is sufficiently large.  This light mode, π, has a wavefunction that is exponentially localized towards the "end" of the chain (the N-th site).  The overlap of this wavefunction with the zeroth site, which is assumed to be coupled to the Higgs, is therefore exponentially suppressed.

The precise mass of the light mode is approximately `m² / (q²ᴺ)`, while the other modes have masses of order `M`. This explains how a light mode with suppressed coupling to the SM sector emerges.

The suppression factor, `q⁻ᴺ`, is the crux of the Clockwork mechanism. By choosing appropriate values for `q` and `N`, one can achieve the desired hierarchy.

## 10. Classical Scale Invariance

## Classical Scale Invariance and the Hierarchy Problem in Principia Metaphysica

The vast hierarchy between the electroweak scale (characterized by the Higgs VEV, *v* ≈ 246.22 GeV) and the Planck scale (M_Pl ≈ 2.435 x 10^18 GeV), a factor of roughly 10^16, represents a profound challenge to particle physics. **1. Mathematical Mechanism: Classical Scale Invariance and Dimensional Transmutation**

Classical Scale Invariance posits that the Lagrangian of a theory possesses no explicit mass scales at the classical (tree) level. This symmetry implies that under a rescaling of spacetime coordinates  *x* → λ*x*, the action remains invariant. This typically requires all mass parameters in the Lagrangian to be set to zero. The Standard Model (SM) Lagrangian explicitly breaks CSI due to the Higgs mass term.

The Coleman-Weinberg (CW) mechanism provides a way to dynamically generate mass scales from a classically scale-invariant theory through quantum corrections.  Consider a massless scalar field *φ* interacting with itself and other fields (e.g., gauge fields). The effective potential, *V_eff(φ)*, including loop corrections, can develop a non-trivial minimum at a non-zero value of *φ*, even if the classical potential is flat. This spontaneous breaking of scale invariance generates a mass scale.

The one-loop effective potential can be expressed in the Landau gauge as:

V_eff(φ) = V_classical(φ) + (ħ / 64π²) [Tr(M_S^4(φ) ln(M_S^2(φ) / μ²) - M_S^4(φ)) + 3 Tr(M_V^4(φ) ln(M_V^2(φ) / μ²) - M_V^4(φ)) - 4 Tr(M_F^4(φ) ln(M_F^2(φ) / μ²) - M_F^4(φ))]

where:

*   *V_classical(φ)* is the classical potential (which is typically zero in a CSI theory).
*   *M_S(φ), M_V(φ), M_F(φ)* are the field-dependent mass matrices for scalars, vectors, and fermions, respectively.
*   *μ* is the renormalization scale.  The explicit dependence on *μ* is removed by renormalization conditions.

The key point is that the *ln(M^2(φ) / μ²)* terms introduce a dependence on the field *φ*. Even if *V_classical(φ) = 0*, the effective potential can develop a non-trivial minimum. The minimum, *φ₀*, determines the dynamically generated mass scale.  The scale *μ* is replaced by the VEV <φ> through dimensional transmutation, a logarithmically slow running of the coupling constants, effectively creating a mass scale from a dimensionless parameter.

## 11. Twin Higgs

## Twin Higgs Mechanism in Principia Metaphysica (PM)

The Principia Metaphysica (PM) framework, with its 26D bosonic string, Sp(2,R) gauge fixing leading to 13D, G2 holonomy compactification, and a large shadow sector, presents a unique context for addressing the Higgs hierarchy problem. The significant discrepancy between the Higgs VEV (v = 246.22 GeV) and the Planck mass (M_Pl = 2.435 x 10^18 GeV) demands an explanation beyond simple topological arguments. This analysis explores the viability of the Twin Higgs mechanism within the PM framework, focusing on its mathematical underpinnings, potential realization within the PM geometry, and the challenges it presents.

**1. Mathematical Mechanism of the Twin Higgs:**

The Twin Higgs mechanism postulates the existence of a "twin" sector that is nearly identical to the Standard Model (SM) but coupled to the SM through a discrete Z2 symmetry.  This symmetry exchanges the SM Higgs doublet, *H*, with a corresponding twin Higgs doublet, *H'*. The key idea is that the Higgs mass term, *m² |H|²*, is protected from quadratic divergences up to a cutoff scale, Λ, by the approximate Z2 symmetry.

Consider a simplified potential:

V(H, H') = λ (|H|² + |H'|²)² + m² (|H|² + |H'|²)

This potential exhibits an SO(8) symmetry (approximately), which is softly broken by smaller terms, potentially arising from radiative corrections or explicit Z2 breaking terms. Crucially, the loop corrections to the Higgs mass now respect the SO(8) symmetry.  This means that the quadratic divergences are not present in the *physical* Higgs mass, but rather in the SO(8) invariant combination.

After electroweak symmetry breaking in both sectors, the Higgs fields acquire VEVs, *v* and *v'*, respectively. The physical Higgs mass, *m_h*, is then related to these VEVs by:

m_h² ≈ λ (v² + v'²)

The observed Higgs VEV, *v*, is typically smaller than *v'*, implying a "little hierarchy" between the two scales. This dilution protects the Higgs mass from large radiative corrections up to a higher scale, Λ, compared to the SM.  The hierarchy is then *partially* explained by the fact that the Higgs mass is suppressed by a factor of v/v' compared to the cutoff scale.

**2. Application to the PM Framework:**

A larger shadow sector could imply a more complex "twin" sector, perhaps with additional fields or interactions.  This could be beneficial in generating a larger *v'*, further diluting the Higgs mass.

*   **Z2 Symmetry:** The crucial aspect is the realization of the Z2 symmetry. In PM, this would likely be implemented through the G2 holonomy compactification on the 7-manifold. The geometry of the 7-manifold, and the way fields are embedded within it, would dictate the symmetry properties of the theory. A specific involution of the 7-manifold could implement the Z2 exchange between the visible and shadow sectors. This involution would need to act on the fields in such a way as to effectively exchange the SM Higgs with its twin counterpart.

*   **Dimensional Chain and Localization:** The dimensional chain (26D -> 13D -> 7D -> 4D) plays a crucial role. The Z2 symmetry could be more manifest at a higher-dimensional level (e.g., in the 13D theory) and then be broken by the compactification process. The localization of the Higgs fields on specific cycles of the 7-manifold would be critical in determining their interactions and symmetry properties. If the SM Higgs and its twin are localized on cycles that are exchanged by the Z2 involution, this would naturally implement the Twin Higgs symmetry.

**3. Specific Calculations (Challenges and Potential Avenues):**

Performing explicit calculations within the PM framework is a significant challenge due to the complexity of the underlying string theory and the G2 holonomy compactification. However, we can outline potential avenues for investigation:

*   **Moduli Stabilization and Z2 Breaking:**  The G2 holonomy compactification leads to moduli fields that parameterize the shape and size of the 7-manifold. *   **Precision Electroweak Constraints:** The Twin Higgs model predicts deviations from the SM predictions for precision electroweak observables, quantified by the S and T parameters. Calculating the S and T parameters within the PM framework would require knowledge of the spectrum of the twin sector and its couplings to the SM. *   **Gauge Coupling Unification:** The Twin Higgs model might impact gauge coupling unification.  The presence of the twin sector could alter the running of the gauge couplings, potentially leading to unification at a different scale or even spoiling unification.  This should be checked.

**4. Honest Assessment of Viability:**

## 12. Technicolor

## Technicolor in Principia Metaphysica: A Viability Assessment

The immense hierarchy between the electroweak scale (v ~ 246 GeV) and the Planck scale (M_Pl ~ 10^18 GeV) remains one of the most pressing problems in modern physics. Within the Principia Metaphysica (PM) framework, characterized by its 26D bosonic string origins, Sp(2,R) gauge fixing to 13D, G2 holonomy compactification to 4D with N=1 SUSY, and a shadow sector, the hierarchy problem is particularly acute. Simple geometric arguments fail to bridge the 16 orders of magnitude gap, and the topological invariant b3=24 alone proves insufficient. This analysis explores Technicolor (TC) as a potential solution, specifically within the context of PM.

**1. Technicolor: A QCD-Like Solution to Electroweak Symmetry Breaking**

Technicolor posits that the Higgs boson is not a fundamental particle but a composite bound state of new fermions ("technifermions") interacting via a new, strong gauge force ("technicolor"). Analogous to QCD, the chiral symmetry breaking of the technifermions at a scale Lambda_TC generates massless Goldstone bosons, three of which are "eaten" by the electroweak gauge bosons (W and Z), providing them with mass. The electroweak VEV, v, is then directly related to Lambda_TC: v ~ F_pi, where F_pi is the technipion decay constant, analogous to the pion decay constant in QCD. To reproduce the observed electroweak scale, we require Lambda_TC ~ 1 TeV.

* **Mathematical Mechanism:**  A typical TC model introduces N_TC technifermions transforming under a new SU(N_TC) gauge group. At a scale Lambda_TC, chiral symmetry breaking occurs:  <psi_L psi_R> ~ Lambda_TC^3.  This generates Goldstone bosons transforming as the adjoint representation of the broken chiral symmetry group.  Electroweak gauge interactions explicitly break the chiral symmetry, leading to a potential for the Goldstone bosons.  Minimizing this potential yields the electroweak VEV: v^2 = F_pi^2 ~ Lambda_TC^2.

* **Application to PM:** In PM, the existence of a shadow sector with 163 degrees of freedom, exceeding the 135 in the visible sector, suggests the possibility of hidden strong dynamics.  These extra degrees of freedom could potentially be technifermions charged under a new gauge force, arising from the complex structure of the 7-manifold resulting from G2 holonomy compactification.

* **Specific Calculations:** While a precise calculation requires specifying the technifermion content and gauge group, we can make a rough estimate. Assuming a QCD-like scaling, F_pi ~ Lambda_TC.  To achieve v ~ 246 GeV, we need Lambda_TC ~ 246 GeV.  This is a crucial constraint.  Determining if the dynamics of the shadow sector can naturally produce such a scale within the PM framework is a significant challenge.  The compactification scale of the 7-manifold, M_G2, must be significantly higher than Lambda_TC to avoid mixing the compactification dynamics with the technicolor dynamics.  This separation of scales is essential.

**2. G2 Associative Cycles and Technicolor Gauge Groups**

The G2 holonomy structure of the 7-manifold offers intriguing possibilities for supporting a technicolor gauge group.  G2 manifolds are characterized by a covariantly constant 3-form Phi.  Associative 3-cycles are calibrated by Phi, meaning that the integral of Phi over the cycle gives the volume. These associative cycles can support gauge fields localized on them.

* **Mathematical Mechanism:**  Consider a D-brane wrapping an associative 3-cycle in the 7-manifold.  The worldvolume theory of the D-brane will contain a gauge theory whose gauge group depends on the number of D-branes wrapping the cycle.  For example, N D-branes wrapping the cycle will give rise to a U(N) gauge theory.  If we assume that this gauge theory becomes strongly coupled at a scale Lambda_TC, it can serve as the technicolor gauge group.

* **Application to PM:** Within the PM framework, the G2 compactification is crucial.  The topology and geometry of the 7-manifold dictate the existence and properties of associative cycles.  If associative cycles exist that can support a gauge group with the appropriate properties (e.g., SU(N_TC) for technicolor), then this offers a compelling connection between the compactification geometry and electroweak symmetry breaking.  The size and shape of the associative cycle influence the gauge coupling and, ultimately, the scale Lambda_TC.  Finding specific cycles with the right properties within the PM compactification is a challenging but potentially rewarding task.

* **Specific Calculations:**  Calculating the gauge coupling on an associative cycle requires knowledge of the metric and the dilaton field on the 7-manifold.  The gauge coupling is inversely proportional to the volume of the cycle and the string coupling.  Therefore, smaller cycles and stronger string coupling lead to larger gauge couplings and potentially a higher Lambda_TC.  Precisely calculating these quantities requires a detailed understanding of the specific G2 compactification in PM.

**3. Extended Technicolor and Fermion Masses**

A major challenge for simple technicolor models is generating fermion masses.  Extended Technicolor (ETC) addresses this by introducing a larger gauge group that contains both the standard model gauge group and the technicolor gauge group.  This extended gauge group is broken at a higher scale, Lambda_ETC >> Lambda_TC, mediating interactions between technifermions and ordinary fermions, generating mass terms.

* **Mathematical Mechanism:**  The ETC gauge group G_ETC is broken to G_SM x G_TC at Lambda_ETC.  This breaking generates massive gauge bosons that mediate four-fermion operators of the form (f bar psi)(psi bar f)/Lambda_ETC^2, where f represents standard model fermions and psi represents technifermions.  These operators, upon condensation of the technifermions, generate masses for the standard model fermions: m_f ~ <psi bar psi>/Lambda_ETC^2 ~ Lambda_TC^3/Lambda_ETC^2.

* **Application to PM:**  The ETC scenario can be incorporated into PM by considering additional branes or fluxes on the 7-manifold that lead to the ETC gauge group.  The location and properties of these branes or fluxes will determine the scale Lambda_ETC.  The shadow sector, with its extra degrees of freedom, might play a crucial role in realizing the ETC mechanism.

* **Specific Calculations:**  Generating realistic fermion masses requires fine-tuning the scale Lambda_ETC.  For example, to generate a top quark mass of around 173 GeV, we need Lambda_ETC ~ (Lambda_TC^3/m_t)^(1/2) ~ (246^3/173)^(1/2) GeV ~ 600 GeV.  This suggests that the ETC scale must be relatively close to the technicolor scale, which can lead to phenomenological challenges.

**4. Fatal Problems: S Parameter and FCNC**

Technicolor models face several serious phenomenological challenges, primarily related to the S parameter and flavor-changing neutral currents (FCNC).

* **S Parameter:** The S parameter is a measure of the electroweak precision constraints. Simple technicolor models predict a large positive S parameter, which is inconsistent with experimental data.  This arises because the technifermions contribute significantly to the vacuum polarization of the electroweak gauge bosons.

* **FCNC:** ETC models often generate FCNC at unacceptably large rates.  This is because the ETC gauge bosons mediate flavor-changing interactions between the standard model fermions.  Suppressing these FCNC requires very high ETC scales, which, in turn, lead to unacceptably small fermion masses.

* **Application to PM:**  In the PM framework, the geometry of the 7-manifold and the properties of the branes and fluxes can potentially suppress the S parameter and FCNC.  For example, specific brane configurations might lead to smaller contributions to the vacuum polarization or suppress the flavor-changing interactions.  However, achieving the required level of suppression is a significant challenge.

**5. Walking/Conformal Technicolor and Compatibility with PM**

Walking/Conformal Technicolor is an attempt to address the S parameter and FCNC problems by modifying the dynamics of the technicolor gauge theory.  In walking technicolor, the technicolor gauge coupling runs slowly (walks) over a wide range of energies, leading to a near-conformal behavior.  This can suppress the S parameter and enhance the technifermion condensate, which can help generate larger fermion masses without requiring excessively high ETC scales.

* **Mathematical Mechanism:**  Walking technicolor is characterized by a large anomalous dimension for the technifermion bilinear operator, gamma_m ~ 1.  This leads to a slow running of the technicolor gauge coupling and a large technifermion condensate, <psi bar psi> ~ Lambda_TC^2 Lambda_ETC.  The large condensate helps generate larger fermion masses, while the slow running suppresses the S parameter.

* **Application to PM:**  Implementing walking technicolor within PM requires finding a 7-manifold geometry and brane configuration that leads to a near-conformal technicolor gauge theory.  This might involve fine-tuning the parameters of the compactification or introducing specific fluxes that affect the running of the technicolor gauge coupling.  The shadow sector could play a crucial role in realizing the walking behavior.

* **Specific Calculations:**  Calculating the anomalous dimension of the technifermion bilinear operator requires a detailed understanding of the technicolor gauge theory.  This is a challenging theoretical problem that often requires non-perturbative techniques.

**Honest Assessment of Viability Today**

Technicolor, in its simplest form, is essentially ruled out by experimental data, primarily due to the S parameter and FCNC constraints.  While walking technicolor offers a potential solution to these problems, it requires fine-tuning and a detailed understanding of the technicolor dynamics.

Within the PM framework, technicolor faces several challenges:

* **Finding Suitable Associative Cycles:**  Identifying associative cycles on the 7-manifold that can support a technicolor gauge group with the correct properties is a non-trivial task.
* **Generating Realistic Fermion Masses:**  Achieving realistic fermion masses while suppressing FCNC requires fine-tuning the ETC scale and the parameters of the compactification.
* **Suppressing the S Parameter:**  Suppressing the S parameter requires either fine-tuning the technicolor dynamics or finding specific brane configurations that lead to smaller contributions to the vacuum polarization.

Despite these challenges, technicolor remains a potentially viable solution to the hierarchy problem within PM.  The shadow sector provides a natural candidate for the technifermions, and the G2 holonomy structure offers a rich geometric framework for realizing the technicolor gauge group.  However, significant theoretical advancements and a deeper understanding of the PM compactification are needed to make technicolor a compelling and experimentally testable theory.
